During the 19th century, certain lighthouse lenses became so powerful that many sailors referred to them as artificial moons. The best known examples were Fresnel lenses, enormous structures made from glass prisms that bent light efficiently without requiring an absurdly thick mass of glass. Some first-order Fresnel lenses stood more than two meters tall and weighed multiple tons while still producing beams visible over 20 miles away.

What made them weirder was transportation. On distant islands and colonial territories, workers hauled these carefully engineered glass structures through deserts, jungles, and steep cliff routes with wooden sledges, oxen, and stubborn determination. Even a single damaged prism could distort the light beam enough to mislead ships. It was like dragging a Victorian laser cannon assembled from chandelier pieces across a mountain path.
