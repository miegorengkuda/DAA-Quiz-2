In the 1800s, some lighthouse lenses were so powerful that sailors called them “artificial moons.” The most famous were Fresnel lenses, giant assemblies of glass prisms designed to bend light efficiently without needing a ludicrously thick chunk of glass. A first-order Fresnel lens could stand over two meters tall and weigh several tons, yet still focus a beam visible from more than 20 miles away.

The weird part is how they were transported. In remote colonies and islands, workers sometimes hauled these precision-engineered glass monuments through jungles, deserts, and cliff paths using wooden sledges, oxen, and pure stubbornness. One damaged prism could distort the beam enough to confuse ships. Imagine carrying a Victorian laser cannon made of chandelier parts across a mountain trail.
